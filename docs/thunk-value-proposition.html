Value Proposition · thunk

Compiling a bright future through open source

Turning what you learn into a public record no one can take back.

thunk does not end with a certificate. It ends with a portfolio you can point to — a device driver you wrote, and real, merged open-source contributions with your name on them. For someone rebuilding after prison, that is the difference between asking to be trusted and showing the proof.

For justice-impacted people in the reentry window, thunk is a systems course that ends in real open-source contributions — verifiable, public proof of skill — where a bootcamp hands you a certificate that only says you attended.

Why open source, for this audience

01A record that does not ask about your past

In open source the review judges the patch, not the person. Contributions are public and merit-judged; nobody on the mailing list asks where you have been.

02Proof you can point to

83% of organizations weigh open-source contributions when they hire. A merged patch outweighs a gap in your history, because it is evidence, not a claim.

03A door, and a community

The work introduces you. Established on-ramps (kernelnewbies, the Linux Kernel Mentorship Program, Outreachy) mean you are not walking in alone.

A certificate says: I showed up. A merged patch says: I did this. It is in the code, my name is on it, and it is still there.

You do not graduate with a promise. You graduate with a history.

Source: 2024 Open Source Software Funding Survey (GitHub, the Linux Foundation, and Harvard): 83% of organizations say open-source contribution is at least somewhat important for prospective hires.
