# thunk — 3-Minute Pitch (spoken)

*Working title. Penn Porterfield. ~420 words, about 3 minutes at a calm pace.
Pause where the paragraphs break. Do not rush the last line.*

---

I taught myself to build a little RPG on a graphing calculator in the honor block of a maximum
security prison. Later I worked through a whole JavaScript book on a locked-down library computer,
because that was the only machine I was allowed to touch. Nobody handed me a curriculum. I put it
together out of scraps. I want to build the thing that would have met me where I was.

Here is the problem. Almost every coding program for people like me teaches web development. The Last
Mile teaches JavaScript. Justice Through Code teaches Python. Good programs, and I respect them. But
web development is the most crowded corner of the whole industry, and it is shrinking. And I could not
find a single prison or reentry program that teaches systems, or embedded, or kernel work. Not one.
There is a reason it is empty: that kind of work usually needs hardware, and hardware does not clear a
prison review.

So I am building thunk. It is one small program, written in Rust, that runs completely offline on a
locked-down laptop. No internet, no install. It teaches how a computer actually works, from true zero,
at your own pace. And it ends with DOOM, the game, booting on a screen you learned to drive from the
bare metal up. The whole low-level stack is simulated in software, so you reach that finish line with
no hardware at all. The thing that kept everyone else out, I designed around.

But here is the part that matters most for reentry. thunk does not end with a certificate. It ends
with a portfolio you can actually point to. You write a real driver. And then, once you are home, you
make your first real contribution to open source. That one is everything, because a merged
contribution is public, it is permanent, and it does not ask about your record. When a background
check might quietly close a door, open source is a door that is already open, and nobody can take it
back. The review judges your code, not your past.

I am not pitching this from the sidelines. I am building the exact capstone it teaches toward, right
now, myself, on real hardware. And the first phase is already built and passing tests. I can show it
running today.

What I am asking for is a yes, and the room to build it. Because this is what I believe we are
actually doing here: compiling a bright future, through open source.
